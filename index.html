Abstract

This paper presents a high-brightness (1200 nits), low-crosstalk (<1.8%) dual-view display based on Micro-LED technology, redefining safety and durability standards for automotive applications. Conventional OLED-based dual-view technologies have shown critical safety limitations due to luminance degradation under harsh environments (85â„ƒ) and burn-in issues caused by static user interfaces.

We overcame these limitations through a proprietary pixel partitioning architecture and a size-independent stamp transfer process capable of large-area scalability. The fabricated prototype demonstrated superior reliability with zero luminance degradation under thermal shock cycling (-40â„ƒ to 85â„ƒ) and high-temperature/high-humidity (85â„ƒ/85%RH) conditions. These results suggest that the proposed Micro-LED display is the only viable reference that fully complies with strict driver distraction regulations such as Euro NCAP while guaranteeing consistent visibility under any environmental condition.

1. Introduction

1.1 The Shift to "Third Living Space"

As autonomous driving technology advances from Level 3 to Level 4, the automotive cockpit is undergoing a paradigm shift from a mere mode of transport to a "Third Living Space." As drivers gain more freedom from vehicle control, there is an essential requirement for **Dual-View** display technology capable of simultaneously providing safety information (Speed, Navigation, Warnings) to the driver (FID) and immersive entertainment content to the passenger (PID). This goes beyond mere convenience; it is becoming a critical safety requirement to ensure that content with different purposes does not visually interfere with one another.

1.2 Limitations of Conventional Technologies

However, commercially available technologies fail to meet the stringent safety and reliability standards required for automotive displays. Conventional LCDs utilizing parallax barriers suffer from significant optical loss and low contrast ratios. OLED-based dual-view technologies, recently gaining attention, possess critical weaknesses for automotive use. First, due to the inherent nature of organic materials, burn-in is inevitable when static UIs are displayed for extended periods. Pixel shift techniques offer only a temporary mitigation. Second, the Auto Brightness Limiter (ABL), which forcibly reduces brightness to manage heat, reacts unexpectedly in bright environments, potentially leading to serious driver visibility issues.

1.3 The Micro-LED Solution

To fundamentally resolve these dilemmas, we propose an inorganic **Micro-LED Dual-View Display**. Inorganic GaN-based emitters maintain ultra-high brightness (>1200 nits) without the risks of burn-in or degradation. In this study, we utilize a proprietary Pixel Partitioning architecture and a scalable stamp transfer process to present a new reference for next-generation cockpit displays.

2. Design & Fabrication

2.1 Optical Architecture & Simulation Methodology

The core challenge in dual-view display design is resolving the trade-off between maximizing View Angle and minimizing Crosstalk. We conducted Monte-Carlo Ray-Tracing simulations using LightTools software to derive the optimal design. Our simulation results identified a process 'Sweet Spot' where crosstalk remains under 2% when the first black matrix (BM1) is offset by +8 Î¼m, the organic coating (OC) is 6 Î¼m, and the second black matrix (BM2) is offset by +3.5 Î¼m.

2.2 Scalable Fabrication: Overcoming the Size Barrier

While previous studies were limited to small prototypes, this study prioritizes **Scalability**. We employed a size-independent stamp transfer process that solves the "seam line" issues of tiling. Furthermore, to address substrate warpage during large-area processing, we introduced a **Vacuum Chuck with Local Flatness Control**. Through this, we successfully controlled transfer position precision to within Â±3 Î¼m even on a 12.3-inch area, proving that uniform image quality can be achieved in large-area displays.

3. Experimental Results

3.1 Optical Performance: Exceeding Automotive Standards

The fabricated 2.1-inch prototype achieved a peak luminance of 1208 nits. This is more than double the brightness of modern automotive OLEDs, which plummet to below 600 nits due to ABL, while reducing power consumption by over 40% compared to LCDs. Crosstalk was measured at less than 1.8%, achieving a 'Ghost-free' state imperceptible to the driver.

3.2 Environmental Reliability: The "Killer App" Feature

We conducted **Torture Tests** (Thermal Shock -40~85â„ƒ) resulting in a 0% dead pixel rate. Under 85â„ƒ/85%RH conditions for 168 hours, luminance degradation was <2% and color shift <0.005. In contrast, an automotive OLED panel exhibited a 22% luminance drop and severe yellowish color shift under identical conditions. This suggests Micro-LED is the *only* solution for high-temp/high-humidity vehicle environments.

4. Discussion

4.1 Comparison with State-of-the-Art

Compared to the latest OLED-based technologies (e.g., BOE), Micro-LED demonstrates overwhelming superiority in 'Consistency.' Unlike OLEDs which dim based on content (APL), Micro-LEDs maintain constant high brightness, a key safety factor for snowy/bright roads. Additionally, high transfer yields (>99.99%) are paving the way for cost competitiveness.

4.2 Regulatory Compliance & Safety

Euro NCAP strongly recommends preventing Driver Distraction. The sub-1.8% crosstalk performance implies that the display itself proactively and perfectly meets safety regulations through its optical design alone, without additional physical barriers.

5. Conclusion

This study demonstrated a Micro-LED dual-view display with high brightness (1200 nits), ultra-low crosstalk (<2%), and automotive-grade reliability.
